Graduate Seminar Reflections

Critical engagement with peer presentations

Graduate Seminar 1: Toby Wren - "Songs For Dead Sailors"

Toby Wren's "Songs For Dead Sailors," described as a "classical composition for jazz musicians," exemplifies creative practice research through its seven-year development process. Drawing initial inspiration from a shipwreck painting in the NGV, Wren expanded beyond visual source material to explore themes of despair, suicide, creative anxiety, and ultimately optimism. The work functions as a song cycle that transforms dark subject matter into something conveying "beauty and wonder," accompanied by projected animations during live performances.

Wren's candid discussion of creative anxiety particularly resonated, especially his description of experiencing panic attacks during his previous album launch. His approach to working through this anxiety by committing to an even more ambitious multimedia project seems counterintuitive but ultimately productive. This parallels my own experience with "RenÃ© Descartes," where taking on philosophical complexity could have overwhelmed the hip-hop framework, but instead created new expressive possibilities.

Most significantly, Wren's integration of extensive research methodology into creative practice demonstrates how scholarly rigor can enhance rather than constrain artistic work. His inclusion of reference lists in musical scores and systematic reading across poetry, philosophy (particularly Camus' "The Myth of Sisyphus"), and other musical works shows how creative practice research can make tacit knowledge explicit. This validates my own approach of combining philosophical research with hip-hop creation as legitimate artistic methodology.

Graduate Seminar 2: Jack Williams - "Sonic Narratives in Music Production"

Jack Williams' PhD research revolutionized my understanding of how music production techniques actively construct narrative meaning beyond lyrics and melody. His investigation into "sonic narratives" demonstrates that mixing and production choices function as storytelling vehicles, capable of conveying character relationships, emotional states, and plot developments through proxemic zones and personic environments. This concept of "mixing as art" validates technical production work as legitimate creative practice.

Williams' analysis of proxemic zones, particularly his examination of Nick Cave and Kylie Minogue's "Where the Wild Roses Grow," revealed how vocal treatment can establish character dynamics within narrative. Cave's distant, "amorphous" presence versus Minogue's intimate, breathy closeness reflects their murderer/victim relationship through purely sonic means. This framework illuminates how my own production choices in "RenÃ© Descartes" affect philosophical content delivery, making me more conscious of how beat selection and vocal processing shape conceptual meaning.

Most transformative was Williams' creative practice methodology of producing alternate versions of ambiguous songs to test how sonic narrative influences interpretation. His examples with Sufjan Stevens' "Casimir Pulaski Day" and Amy Winehouse's "I Heard Love Is Blind" demonstrate that identical lyrics can convey completely different emotional narratives through production choices alone. This validates experimental approaches to hip-hop production where the same philosophical concepts could be expressed through vastly different sonic treatments, opening new possibilities for how meaning emerges from the complex interplay between content and sound.

Graduate Seminar 3: Katherine Marchingo - "The Tea to Busan: Creating Art in a Falling World"

Katherine Marchingo's presentation revolutionized my understanding of producing as creative practice rather than merely administrative support. Her central question, "How does a producer approach creating art in a world that's falling apart?" reframes producing as an art form with legitimate creative methodology. This resonates powerfully with my own experience navigating between technical production skills and artistic vision in hip-hop creation.

Marchingo's concept of "polycrisis," the causal entanglement of multiple simultaneous crises, fundamentally shifted my perspective on creative responsibility. Her research pivot from climate change to "leadership in polycrisis" demonstrates how artists must acknowledge that different communities face different primary concerns. This mirrors my experience creating "RenÃ© Descartes," where philosophical authenticity had to be balanced against hip-hop's diverse cultural contexts and expectations.

Most transformative was her "revolutionary ant theory" from complexity science: sometimes changing the world requires being the one individual who breaks colony rules. Without rule-breakers, the colony dies. This validates my experimental approach to merging Cartesian philosophy with hip-hop, taking risks with unexpected juxtapositions rather than staying within established genres. Her framework of "mapping impossible dreams" through place, people, ideas, planning, and business provides a practical methodology for pursuing conceptually ambitious projects.

Marchingo's emphasis on place as "major collaborator" particularly connects to my understanding of creative context. Just as her work acknowledges Country and cultural positioning, my "RenÃ© Descartes" project emerges from specific academic and hip-hop communities that shape its meaning. Her student Manal's letter conclusion, "Your voice might only be a note in a piano piece, but that one note is enough to change it all," encapsulates the power of individual creative acts to generate transformative ripples, validating the experimental work we undertake in this course.
